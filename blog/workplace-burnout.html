Workplace Stress

When Work Breaks You: Recognising Burnout Before It Takes Over

Roy Lam
MBACP Registered Therapist
7 March 2026 🕒 5 min read

You wake up already tired. You get through the day, but only just. By evening you have nothing left, and somewhere along the way, you stopped being able to remember what you used to enjoy about your work. If any of that sounds familiar, this post is for you.

Burnout is not just tiredness

Most people who come to me in the grip of burnout have spent months, sometimes years, telling themselves they just need a good night’s sleep, a holiday, or a quieter week. The problem is that burnout is not tiredness you can sleep off. It is a state of chronic depletion, emotional numbness, and disconnection that builds gradually, often invisibly, until the body or mind calls a halt.

The World Health Organisation now classifies burnout as an occupational phenomenon, characterised by three things: exhaustion, growing detachment from your work, and a sense that you are no longer effective at what you do. Notice that only one of those three is physical. The other two are psychological, which is exactly why rest alone rarely resolves it.

What the signs actually look like

Burnout rarely announces itself clearly. More often, it creeps in through the back door:

  • You feel flat, not just tired, even about things that used to energise you
  • Small tasks feel enormous, and your tolerance for frustration has dropped sharply
  • You are increasingly cynical about your work, your colleagues, or your organisation
  • You feel detached, going through the motions without really being present
  • Your body is sending signals: headaches, disrupted sleep, recurring illness, tension you cannot shift
  • You have stopped talking about it because you no longer expect anything to change

One of the most telling signs I see in clients is the absence of hope. Not despair exactly, just a quiet flatness: a sense that this is simply how things are now.

Burnout is not a personal failing. It is what happens when a person is asked to give more than they can sustainably give, for long enough.

Why high achievers are often hit hardest

There is a particular kind of person who tends to arrive at burnout later than most, and harder. They are conscientious, capable, and take their responsibilities seriously. They set high standards for themselves. They find it difficult to ask for help or to say no. And crucially, they have learned to interpret pushing through as a virtue rather than a warning sign.

If that sounds like you, it is worth noticing that the same qualities that have made you effective are the ones that may have kept you running long past the point where something needed to change. Burnout does not happen to people who do not care. It happens to people who care deeply, and who have been carrying too much for too long.

The role of the environment

It is important to say clearly: burnout is not a personal failing. The TUC reported earlier this year that nearly 8 in 10 workplace safety representatives now cite stress as the primary hazard in their workplace, the highest proportion ever recorded. Millions of working days are being lost each year to work-related stress, depression, and anxiety across the UK. These are structural conditions, not individual ones.

That does not mean the individual is powerless. But it does mean that recovery from burnout often requires more than personal coping strategies. It requires a genuine examination of what has been asked of you, what you have been asking of yourself, and what needs to shift.

What therapy offers

When clients come to me experiencing burnout, we rarely start by talking about work tactics or time management. Instead, we start with something more fundamental: what does this person actually need, and what has been getting in the way of them having it?

Therapy offers a space to slow down and look honestly at what is happening, without the pressure to fix it immediately or perform okayness. Over time, that might involve exploring the beliefs and patterns that have driven overworking, rebuilding a relationship with rest and self-worth that is not contingent on productivity, or simply having a place to grieve what the last few years have cost you.

Recovery from burnout is real and it is possible. But it tends to require a different kind of attention than the one that got you here.
